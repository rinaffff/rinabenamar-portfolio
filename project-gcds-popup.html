Case Study 03 · Visual Merchandising & Retail Strategy

GCDS — Pop-Up Stores

A retail concept imagining four simultaneous GCDS pop-up stores across four cities — each adapted to its local cultural context while remaining instantly recognisable as one brand.

Client

GCDS

Sector

Fashion Retail

Role

Visual Merchandiser & Concept Designer

Deliverables

Concept, Planograms, 3D Renders

Fig. 0 — Concept Visual Four Cities, One Brand
Context & Brief

One drop, four cities, four cultural readings.

GCDS is known for its maximalist, playful, internet-native identity — a brand whose Milanese energy reads differently in every market it touches. The brief: design a simultaneous pop-up store rollout across four international cities for a single collection drop, with each location's window and interior concept responding to local cultural references, while every store remains unmistakably GCDS.

This meant building a retail framework flexible enough to be "translated" city by city — without diluting the brand's core identity into something generic.

Role & Responsibilities
  • — Conducted market and competitor analysis for each target city to identify culturally resonant visual references.
  • — Concepted the Milan flagship window: a Barilla × GCDS collaboration staged as an oversized pink pasta box installation in Piazza del Duomo.
  • — Concepted the Paris pop-up window around a Playmobil-inspired display system, referencing French toy culture with a luxury twist.
  • — Produced 2D planograms and 3D spatial renders translating each concept into a buildable retail environment.
  • — Maintained a consistent GCDS visual language (colour, type, fixtures) across all four city-specific concepts.
Creative Process

Research first, surrealism second.

Each city began with a cultural mapping exercise: what local references would feel like a wink to residents, rather than a tourist cliché? Milan's culinary icon Barilla became a surrealist object — an oversized pink pasta box doubling as a pop-up structure in the city's most iconic square. Paris, meanwhile, leaned into nostalgic French toy culture via a Playmobil-referencing window display — a way of being playful without being predictable.
